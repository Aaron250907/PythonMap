Portugal

Portugal – Lower / Medium Corruption

Portugal is generally seen as a relatively clean and stable democracy, but it still faces corruption challenges, especially in politics, local government, and public contracts. Its CPI score is around the low to mid-60s, placing it ahead of many countries but behind the very best performers in Europe.

Main Causes of Corruption

  • Political scandals: Cases involving party funding, local councils, or public officials can undermine trust in institutions.
  • Complex bureaucracy: Complicated procedures in planning, licensing, and public contracts can create opportunities for favouritism or informal deals.
  • Public procurement risks: Large construction or infrastructure projects may be vulnerable if oversight is weak or bidding processes are unclear.
  • Limited whistleblower culture (historically): Fear of retaliation and social pressure made it difficult for people to report wrongdoing, although this is improving.

Impacts on Society & Ethics

  • Reduced trust in politics: Repeated scandals can make citizens feel that “all politicians are corrupt,” which discourages participation and voting.
  • Economic inefficiency: If contracts are awarded unfairly, taxpayers might pay more for lower-quality services.
  • Perception of unfairness: When powerful individuals avoid consequences, people may feel that justice is not applied equally.
  • Ethical tension: Most public servants are honest, but a few highly public cases can damage the reputation of the whole system.

Anti-Corruption Strategies

  • Whistleblower protection laws: New laws inspired by EU directives give better protection to people who report corruption.
  • Judicial independence: Courts can investigate and prosecute high-level officials, strengthening rule of law.
  • Transparency in public spending: Many government contracts and budgets are published online, allowing journalists and citizens to monitor how money is used.
  • Cooperation with EU institutions: EU oversight and anti-corruption mechanisms help Portugal align with best practices.

Ethical Reflection

Ethically, Portugal aims to uphold integrity, equality before the law, and responsible use of public funds. However, when individual leaders act selfishly, they break the ethical contract between government and citizens. The challenge is not only to punish corrupt behaviour but to create a culture where honesty, transparency, and accountability are normal and expected.

India

India – Medium Corruption

India is the world’s largest democracy with a vibrant media, active civil society, and many laws designed to fight corruption. At the same time, its CPI score in the high 30s reflects ongoing challenges with bribery, bureaucracy, and political funding.

Main Causes of Corruption

  • Large bureaucracy: Complex paperwork for licences, certificates, permits, and land registration can encourage “speed money” to move files faster.
  • Political financing: Election campaigns are expensive, and funding is not always fully transparent, which can create expectations of favours in return.
  • Poverty and inequality: When people struggle to access basic services, they may feel forced to pay bribes or accept unfair treatment.
  • Uneven enforcement: Some states and departments are stricter than others, which sends mixed signals about what is acceptable.

Impacts on Society & Ethics

  • Loss of public funds: Money lost through corruption could have been invested in schools, hospitals, clean water, or infrastructure.
  • Everyday bribery: Ordinary people may face demands for unofficial payments to get birth certificates, driving licences, or police reports.
  • Erosion of trust: When high-profile corruption cases are exposed, people may feel that justice only applies to the poor, not to powerful individuals.
  • Unfair opportunities: When jobs, contracts, or marks are given based on connections or bribes instead of merit, talented people are discouraged.

Anti-Corruption Strategies

  • Right to Information Act (RTI): Allows citizens to request information from government offices, increasing transparency.
  • Digital services: Online portals for taxes, licences, and payments reduce face-to-face contact, which lowers opportunities for bribery.
  • Lokpal and Lokayuktas: Independent ombudsman bodies were created to investigate corruption complaints against public officials.
  • Active media and civil society: Investigative journalism, anti-corruption campaigns, and social media activism expose unethical behaviour.

Ethical Reflection

Ethically, India has strong values in its constitution, including justice, equality, and dignity for all. The tension between these ideals and real-life corruption creates frustration but also motivation for reform. Students, journalists, lawyers, and activists play a critical role in demanding integrity from leaders and refusing to normalize bribery in daily life.

Pakistan

Pakistan – Higher Corruption

Pakistan faces more severe corruption challenges, with CPI scores in the high 20s. Political instability, weak institutions, and patronage networks have allowed corruption to become deeply rooted in many sectors. However, there are also strong voices pushing for accountability and reform, especially among younger generations.

Main Causes of Corruption

  • Patronage politics: Jobs, contracts, and favours may be distributed based on personal loyalty rather than merit.
  • Weak institutional independence: Political interference in the police, courts, and accountability bodies reduces their ability to act fairly.
  • Economic pressures: Low wages for public officials and limited economic opportunities can make bribery more tempting or even seen as “normal.”
  • Conflict and instability: Security challenges and frequent political changes shift focus away from long-term institutional reform.

Impacts on Society & Ethics

  • Serious loss of public money: Funds intended for education, electricity, healthcare, or flood protection may be misused or stolen.
  • Poverty and inequality: Corruption makes it harder for poor communities to access quality services and fair treatment.
  • Brain drain: Skilled professionals may emigrate because of limited opportunities and unfair systems.
  • Public distrust: When corruption cases are not prosecuted consistently, people lose faith in the fairness of the system.

Anti-Corruption Strategies

  • National Accountability Bureau (NAB): A central body investigating corruption cases involving public officials and large financial crimes.
  • Digitisation efforts: E-governance projects, online tax filing, and digitised land records aim to reduce direct contact and opportunities for bribery.
  • Civil society activism: Journalists, lawyers, student groups, and NGOs raise awareness and demand reforms.
  • International pressure: Cooperation with global bodies encourages better regulations on money laundering and financial transparency.

Ethical Reflection

Ethically, Pakistan’s struggle with corruption raises questions about justice, responsibility, and leadership. People who engage in corrupt acts often justify it as “the only way to get things done,” but this thinking harms those who are already vulnerable. Long-term change depends on strengthening institutions and empowering citizens who insist on fairness, honesty, and the rule of law.

Comparative Analysis

Looking at Portugal, India, and Pakistan together shows that corruption is shaped by history, politics, economics, and culture. No country is completely free from corruption, but the level and type of corruption can be very different.

The comparison suggests that strong, independent institutions, transparent processes, and a culture of integrity are key to reducing corruption. Wealth and development help, but they are not enough on their own; ethical leadership and active citizens are also essential.

Anti-Corruption Solutions & Ethical Actions

Fighting corruption is not just the job of governments. Businesses, schools, communities, and individuals all share responsibility for building a fair and honest society.

Global Solutions

  • Strengthen the rule of law: Courts and police must be independent and able to apply rules fairly.
  • Increase transparency: Budgets, contracts, and decisions should be publicly available and easy to understand.
  • Reduce red tape: Simpler processes mean fewer chances for officials to demand bribes.
  • Protect whistleblowers: People who report wrongdoing must be safe from retaliation.
  • Support free media: Journalists should be able to investigate and publish corruption stories without fear.

Role of Governments

  • Create and enforce strong anti-corruption laws.
  • Use technology to track public spending and reduce cash transactions.
  • Ensure recruitment and promotions are based on merit, not connections.
  • Involve citizens in monitoring local projects and budgets.

What Citizens & Students Can Do

  • Refuse to pay or accept bribes, even when it seems “normal.”
  • Report unethical behaviour when it is safe to do so.
  • Join or support organisations that promote transparency and human rights.
  • Use social media responsibly to raise awareness, not to spread rumours.
  • Practice integrity in school: no cheating, plagiarism, or unfair advantages.

Ethical Takeaway

Corruption is a powerful reminder that laws alone are not enough. A fair society depends on people choosing to act ethically even when nobody is watching. By studying real examples from Portugal, India, and Pakistan, we can see the consequences of corrupt choices—and the hope that comes from honest leadership and courageous citizens.
